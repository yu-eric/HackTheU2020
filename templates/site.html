What is COVID-19?

COVID-19, or the 2019 novel coronavirus (SARS-CoV2), is a highly infectious virus that infects the respiratory tract of a patient. Symptoms typically include that of what one might expect with a cold or just general discomfort/nausea.

COVID-19 can lead to pneumonia, which is the direct result of the buildup of fluid in the lungs. This fluid can appear on x-rays as a symptom of pneumonia, which can be fatal if left untreated for long enough or if there are pre-existing symptoms in the patient. The long-term impacts of infection have yet to be seen on individuals without pre-existing conditions, but individuals with pre-existing are at a substantial risk because of the nature of COVID-19 to lead to pneumonia.

The best thing an individual can do at the moment is to perform social distancing (i.g. keeping physical distance to a radius of 6 feet) and wear a mask in public spaces. Gatherings should be limited to as few as possible as well to limit the exposure to other individuals.

Without these preventative measures and given the infection rate of COVID-19, after 10 generations of infections, an individual would have been at least in part responsible to over 50,000 infections, compared to the flu's 20. This was roughly calculated using the COVID-19 infection rate of 3 per person and the influenza infection rate of 1.4 per person.

About COVID-19 x-ray verification

In order to accurately pinpoint malignant and benign growths, Covision uses a reinforcement machine learning (ML) algorithm. The role of computing in medicine has grown exponentially over the last few decades with the advent of more robust ML algorithms leading to results such as the case of Covision.

By taking a dataset of thousands of x-rays taken by doctors, we are able to have our algorithm distinguish with a range of certainty the given x-ray is a result of COVID-19.

Most of the time, x-rays must be observed for greater periods of time in detail by practitioners in order to gauge the severity of an infection, but a machine learning algorithm can cut this time down drastically to maximize the time a patient is being treated.

Look at the Data

The dataset is publicly avaiable at the ieee github page Our Process

After downloading the dataset, we had a wide number of images of different lung x-rays, some of which were COVID-19, while others were bacterial or not infections at all. We labeled this data in Google Cloud using the provided specifications.

From here, we were able to create a classification in Google Cloud's AutoML image classification platform to assist in putting each image in computer "readable" form in order to create the model that helps you determine if an image is COVID-19!
